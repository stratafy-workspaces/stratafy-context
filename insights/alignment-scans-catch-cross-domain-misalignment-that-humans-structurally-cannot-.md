# Alignment scans catch cross-domain misalignment that humans structurally cannot — this is the defensible AI-native moat

## Summary
The STIR scans proved that AI holding full foundation context while evaluating each strategy catches misalignment that no human process would find. The "does fundraising protect craft values?" question is cross-domain and structurally invisible to traditional review. This is the AI-native moat — not speed, but a fundamentally different analytical capability.

## Description
The alignment scan consistently flagged the same foundational gap across multiple strategies in a way that would never surface in traditional strategy review processes.

The specific pattern: "Craft" is STIR's second-ranked value, yet 5 of 7 strategies have zero mechanisms to protect it during execution. No human reviewer would audit fundraising readiness and ask "does this protect your craft values?" — it's a cross-domain question that requires holding the full foundation context while evaluating each strategy.

This is the defensible moat: the AI holds the complete foundation (mission + vision + 6 values + 6 beliefs + 6 principles) in working memory while evaluating each strategy. Humans can't do this reliably — they forget values, they don't check beliefs against every decision, they lose track of principles under execution pressure.

The proof point for the Sequoia pitch: "A traditional strategy review would have scored STIR's fundraising strategy as solid. Our engine scored it 7.8 and identified that it doesn't protect their core value of craft excellence — a gap that, left unaddressed, would undermine the very quality that makes them fundable."

This is the "AI-native advantage" we keep talking about — it's not just faster, it's a fundamentally different capability.
