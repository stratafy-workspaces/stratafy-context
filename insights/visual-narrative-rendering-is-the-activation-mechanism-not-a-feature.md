# Visual narrative rendering is the activation mechanism, not a feature

## Summary
The effort to turn scattered strategy into something presentable is so high that most people never do it — so strategy stays invisible and urgency to maintain it never builds. Beautiful, narrative rendering of the workspace is what breaks this. Without it, the infrastructure doesn't become real to the people who need to care about it.

## Description
The core adoption problem with any strategy infrastructure tool is not whether it's technically capable — it's whether the people who need to care about it ever feel its value viscerally.

Currently the workspace exists but renders as a database. The gap between raw workspace data and something that creates emotional resonance — urgency, pride, clarity — is enormous. And because that gap exists, most people don't maintain their workspace, because they never see the payoff.

The flywheel that needs to exist:
1. Build workspace → 2. Render it beautifully, tell the story compellingly → 3. Client feels the value viscerally → 4. They want to keep it current → 5. Workspace improves → 6. Story gets better → 7. Urgency compounds.

Right now the loop breaks at step 2. Visual narrative rendering — audience modes, story flows, beautiful strategy tree — is the step that closes the loop.

This has a direct implication for onboarding: the first time a client sees their own strategy rendered and narrated back to them, in a form that's actually presentable to their board or investors, is the moment they become a real user. Not the setup, not the training — that moment.

Product implication: the strategy storytelling view (audience modes, talk track flows) should be prioritised not as a demo feature but as the primary onboarding experience. Build someone's workspace → immediately render it in story mode → walk them through their own strategy in the investor or team view → that's the activation moment.

This also reframes what 'product quality' means for Stratafy. It's not just whether the data model is correct. It's whether the rendered output is good enough that someone would want to show it in a meeting. That bar is significantly higher than most infrastructure tools accept.
